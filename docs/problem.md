# The Problem

> _Tens of millions of working professionals know AI is reshaping their job and feel they're falling behind. The options to "catch up" are all broken._

---

## 1. The five things that are true at the same time

1. **AI is reshaping every knowledge job.** Marketers, PMs, lawyers, designers, engineers, ops, founders — the daily workflow is changing in real time.
2. **AI moves faster than any course can.** Models, tools, patterns, frameworks, and best practices change every week. A "12-week course" is obsolete by week 4.
3. **People feel guilty.** They scroll Twitter, save 50 articles, watch one YouTube, abandon a $300 cohort, never finish the bootcamp. Net knowledge gained: low. Net anxiety: high.
4. **Time is the scarce resource, not desire.** A working PM has 5–15 minutes between meetings, not three uninterrupted hours on Saturday morning.
5. **Theory without building rots; building without theory misleads.** Every existing format does one but not both.

The combination produces what we call **"AI FOMO"** — a chronic, low-grade fear that *the field is leaving you behind*. It's the dominant emotion of a working professional in 2026.

## 2. Who feels it most

| Persona | The exact pain |
|---|---|
| **Working PM, "I want to be a builder"** | Sees AI features ship around them. Wants to vibe-code, but doesn't know where to start. Has a day job. Feels they've lost their builder edge. |
| **Mid-career engineer** | Ship-velocity is up thanks to AI tools. But the field is moving faster than they can read. Knows there's a frontier they're not on. |
| **Senior architect** | Has the experience to evaluate what's hype. Doesn't have the time to test 30 new tools a quarter. Wants distilled insight, not opinions. |
| **Curious starter** (any age) | Watches their friends "get into AI." Tried ChatGPT. Tried a YouTube tutorial. Lost interest. Never had something that felt like *daily progress*. |
| **Researcher / frontier engineer** | Already on the bleeding edge. Wants discoveries from peers, not noise. Wants to *teach* and build a public reputation. Wants a place that isn't Twitter. |
| **Kid (8–14) interested in AI** | Wants something fun. Existing curriculum is either too corporate or assumes high school CS. There's no "Khan Academy for AI" that's *playful enough*. |
| **Recruiter / hiring manager** | Resumes are a lie in 2026. Github stars are a poor signal. Wants to find people who *actually shipped* something specific, recently. |

## 3. Why the existing options don't work

### Twitter / X / LinkedIn feed

- **Wins:** real-time, free.
- **Loses:** zero curriculum, infinite firehose, optimized for engagement not learning. You feel busy without progressing.

### YouTube

- **Wins:** depth, free.
- **Loses:** 60-minute videos when you have 7. No personalization. No memory. No state across sessions.

### Coursera / Udemy / DeepLearning.AI

- **Wins:** structured, credentialed.
- **Loses:** stale by week 4 in AI. Static. No "where am I now" sense. Completion rates are famously bad (<10%).

### Bootcamps (Lambda School, Maven cohorts, etc.)

- **Wins:** community, accountability.
- **Loses:** $1k–$10k. Cohort-paced. Can't fit around a day job. End at a fixed date.

### Vendor academies (Anthropic / OpenAI / Hugging Face)

- **Wins:** authoritative for that vendor.
- **Loses:** vendor-locked. No personalization. No social. No "where do I go next" beyond their funnel.

### Newsletters (Import AI, The Rundown, Latent Space, Ben's Bites)

- **Wins:** signal-rich, fast.
- **Loses:** linear, no path, no personalization, no progress, no doing.

### Stack Overflow / Reddit / Discord

- **Wins:** humans answering humans.
- **Loses:** fragmented, hostile, slow, unsorted, no ramp.

### Duolingo

- **Wins:** the gamified bite-size shape we love.
- **Loses:** wrong domain. Languages don't change. AI does, weekly.

**No existing product does what we're describing.** The closest cousins (Brilliant.org, Duolingo) inspired the *shape* of the experience but were built for static knowledge. AI's real-time evolution requires a *cognition layer* that none of them have.

## 4. The structural problem under all of this

Education for fast-moving fields needs four things at once:

1. **Bite-size delivery** — fits the cracks of a working day.
2. **Personalized path** — what *you* need next, given what you already know.
3. **Real-time content** — refreshed weekly, not yearly.
4. **Practical doing** — try it, ship it, see it work.

Every existing product picks 1 or 2 of these. Bootcamps do (2)+(4). YouTube does (4) but not (1) or (2). Coursera does (1) but not (3) or (4). Twitter does (3) but nothing else.

The reason no product has all four is that **all four require AI to make the unit economics work**. Personalizing 10,000 paths weekly with frontier-level content used to cost $50/user/year in editor labor. With models that can extract, evaluate, and generate at $0.20/user/year, the math finally works.

## 5. Our hypothesis

> **The product that does all four — bite-size, personalized, current, practical — wins this moment in education.** It will become as dominant in AI learning as Duolingo became in languages, with a much bigger TAM (every knowledge worker on Earth feels AI FOMO).

We then go further than Duolingo could, by adding:

- **Memory** (an evolving cognitive profile of each learner).
- **Social** (follow others, contribute, see what they ship).
- **Hiring graph** (the platform becomes the new LinkedIn for technical roles, by accident).
- **Open source forking** (the same engine spawns a new generation of community-driven micro-learning across many fast-moving domains).

## 6. The wedge

Don't try to do all of it on day one. Start with one segment and one wow:

- **Segment:** working AI builders + curious starters.
- **Wow:** "I have 5 minutes between meetings. LearnAI just taught me prompt caching with a working code snippet I can paste into Claude Code right now."

Earn that wow first. Layer the social, hiring, and forking on top once retention is proven.
